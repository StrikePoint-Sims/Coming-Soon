Trackman iO in Eastern Connecticut

StrikePoint Sims is opening Fall 2026 in Colchester, Connecticut with three bays equipped with Trackman iO launch monitors. The same dual-radar technology used on the PGA Tour and in high-end fitting studios. Here's what that means in practice.

What Trackman iO actually measures

Trackman iO is a ceiling-mounted system that uses two radar units to track both the club and the ball simultaneously, from the moment of impact through the full arc of ball flight. It measures more than 30 data points per swing, including:

Ball Speed
Velocity off the clubface at impact
Launch Angle
Initial vertical angle of ball flight
Spin Rate
Total RPM including backspin and sidespin
Spin Axis
Tilt that determines curve and shot shape
Carry & Total Distance
Measured flight, not projected from a model
Club Speed
How fast the club is moving at impact
Club Path
Direction the club is traveling through the strike
Attack Angle
Vertical direction of the club at impact
Face Angle
Where the face is pointing at moment of impact
Smash Factor
Efficiency of contact (ball speed divided by club speed)

The radar tracks the ball from impact through the ceiling, measuring actual flight rather than projecting it from a model. Accuracy is within ±1 mph ball speed, the same tolerance cited in PGA Tour data collection standards. When the system reports a number, it measured it. It didn't estimate it.

How it compares to other simulator technology

Golf simulator technology generally falls into three categories. Understanding the differences matters if you're using a simulator to actually improve your game rather than just hit balls somewhere warm.

Single-radar systems
Track either the ball or the club, not both simultaneously. Ball data or club data is measured directly; the other is estimated or inferred. Accurate at what they measure directly. The gap shows when you're trying to understand what your club is doing at impact.
Camera-only and photometric systems
Use high-speed cameras and light sensors to capture the impact zone and estimate ball behavior from imagery. Common in consumer simulators. Work well under optimal conditions. Results can vary with lighting, mat wear, and the quality of the detection model. Significant improvements over the past several years, but fundamentally different from radar-based tracking.
Dual-radar systems (Trackman iO)
Track both club and ball in full flight, simultaneously. Weather-independent: radar works the same in a bay as on the range. No surface conditions to affect readings. Measures what it reports rather than estimating it. The standard for professional fitting, tour player practice, and serious amateur development.

The cost difference between categories is significant. Dual-radar systems are substantially more expensive than camera-based alternatives, which is why you see them in high-end fitting studios and on professional tours rather than in entertainment-focused venues. StrikePoint is built for people who actually play, and the equipment reflects that.

Why we chose Trackman iO for StrikePoint

Two reasons.

First, the data integrity. If you're using a simulator to improve your game, the quality of the feedback determines the value of the session. Trackman gives you numbers you can trust, compare session to session, and share with a teaching professional. Many of the PGA teaching pros we're planning to partner with for optional in-bay lessons already use Trackman as their primary analysis tool. The continuity matters: your numbers from StrikePoint carry meaning outside the bay.

Second, the course software. Trackman's Performance Studio includes over 300 courses, built at photorealistic quality with accurate green contours and firmness data. Playing a course on Trackman isn't the same experience as a lower-fidelity system. For a golfer who has played real courses, the difference is noticeable. The simulation is good enough to be meaningful practice, not just entertainment.
